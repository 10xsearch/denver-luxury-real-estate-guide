Denver luxury market at a glance in 2026

Denver’s broader market is rebalancing, and the luxury tier is doing so on its own terms. In February 2026 the metro recorded 2,702 closed listings, an overall median price near $575,000, and active inventory of roughly 9,023 homes, up about 9 percent year over year.[2] That rising supply matters for high end buyers because it restores negotiating room that was scarce in tighter years. Within the luxury segment, the metro luxury median sat near $1.94 million in early 2026, down about 4.3 percent from the prior year, and detached luxury listings and pending sales rose while attached luxury demand softened.[1] The headline is simple: there is no single Denver luxury number, and conditions diverge sharply by neighborhood, price band, and property type.

The best luxury neighborhoods in Denver

The strongest luxury submarkets are Cherry Creek and Cherry Creek North for walkable, low maintenance living; Cherry Hills Village and Greenwood Village for gated estates on large lots; Washington Park, Bonnie Brae, and Belcaro for classic single family architecture near the park; and Hilltop and Crestmoor for a central estate feel with strong rebuild inventory. Cherry Creek and Cherry Creek North

Cherry Creek is the address most buyers picture when they imagine luxury in Denver, and it is the city’s most liquid high end submarket. The walkable retail core and the Cherry Creek North dining and gallery scene keep demand strong year round, and the area set records in 2026: 78 condos and townhomes sold at an average of $2.08 million, with luxury product averaging about $896 per square foot and new construction pushing past $1,000 per square foot.[8] Single family pricing runs from roughly $2 million to well over $10 million, while the overall Cherry Creek median sits near $1.4 million to $1.5 million depending on the month and source.[3] Secure parking, private outdoor space, and quality finishes command the strongest premiums, and well priced, move in ready inventory rarely sits.

Cherry Hills Village and Greenwood Village

For estate buyers, Cherry Hills Village is Denver’s benchmark, with gated privacy and large lots. Typical values run around $3.25 million, with listing medians above $4 million and estates extending past $10 million.[4][5] Greenwood Village offers a similar estate feel with strong corporate proximity to the Denver Tech Center, a median around $1.6 million to $1.8 million, and new construction ranging from roughly $1.2 million for an infill home to more than $4 million for a full rebuild on a large lot.[7] Both are lower volume, higher stakes submarkets where accurate pricing against a thin set of genuinely comparable sales matters far more than broad exposure, and where privacy and timeline protection are often as important to sellers as the headline price. Buyers who care about specific school assignments should research them directly, as covered in the schools section below.

Washington Park, Bonnie Brae, and Belcaro

Washington Park is defined by classic architecture, mature tree lined streets, and one of the best urban parks in the country at its doorstep. The neighborhood is highly competitive, with a median near $1.7 million and homes selling in roughly 15 days during spring 2026.[6] Bonnie Brae and Belcaro, just south and east, offer quieter pockets of custom and rebuilt homes. These areas reward patient, well advised buyers, because a renovated or new build single family home within a few blocks of the park can meaningfully outperform comparable square footage further out. Understanding which blocks and orientations carry a premium is the difference between a fair deal and overpaying.

Hilltop, Crestmoor, and the central estate pockets

Hilltop and Crestmoor blend an estate sensibility with a central location and a deep inventory of mid century homes, many of which are being renovated or scraped and rebuilt as modern luxury. Hilltop pricing generally runs from about $1.7 million to $2.3 million and has been appreciating, while in Crestmoor finished new builds can reach $3 million to $4 million and beyond.[11] In all of these pockets the rebuild versus renovate decision drives value, so a buyer or seller who understands current construction costs and what the market will actually pay for new product holds a decisive edge.

How much does a luxury home cost in Denver?

Luxury is defined relative to each submarket, not by a single citywide number. In early 2026 the Denver metro luxury median was near $1.94 million, down about 4.3 percent year over year.[1] Entry condos in Cherry Creek start near $700,000, single family homes there run from $2 million to well over $10 million[3], and Cherry Hills Village estates begin in the mid $3 million range and extend past $10 million.[4][5] For a county wide view of price bands by area, the Denver County Price Guide is a useful reference.[10] Because these bands move month to month, an address specific comparison is far more reliable than an average.

Financing a luxury purchase: jumbo loans in Denver

Most Denver luxury purchases involve a jumbo loan. For 2026 the conforming loan limit across the Denver metro counties rose to $862,500 for a single family home, and any mortgage above that threshold is a jumbo loan.[9] Jumbo financing typically carries stricter requirements, including larger down payments, deeper reserve requirements, and more documentation, though competitive programs exist for qualified buyers. Buying a Denver luxury home: new construction versus resale

The rebuild trend runs through Denver’s best neighborhoods, so many buyers weigh a renovated resale against new construction. New construction offers contemporary systems and finishes, but it carries cost and timeline risk, and in areas like Greenwood Village it ranges from roughly $1.2 million for an infill build to over $4 million for a large rebuild.[7] Resale homes can offer mature lots and established blocks at a lower entry point, with renovation upside. The right choice depends on the neighborhood, current construction costs, and how the market is pricing new product versus updated classics. A local agent who tracks both sides can tell you where the value actually sits this quarter, the kind of read covered in current market analysis.[12]

A buyer’s checklist for Denver luxury

High end transactions carry diligence that entry level purchases do not. This checklist covers the items that most often surface in Denver luxury deals.

  • Get fully underwritten, not just pre qualified, and prepare proof of funds before touring, since a credible position is decisive in a luxury negotiation.
  • Plan for jumbo terms above the $862,500 conforming limit, including a larger down payment, deeper cash reserves, and more documentation.
  • Budget for a possible appraisal gap, because jumbo appraisals on unique estates can come in below the contract price.
  • Order estate specific inspections, including a sewer scope, structural review, and systems testing on older mid century homes.
  • On large lots, confirm the survey or improvement location certificate, well and septic status, and any water rights.
  • Review the HOA or covenant and architectural review rules early, especially in Cherry Hills Village and Greenwood Village.

Selling a Denver luxury home: private versus open market

Often a sequence works best. A discreet pre market phase tests pricing and reaches motivated buyers without overexposing the home, followed by a coordinated open market launch with professional media, targeted digital placement, and direct outreach to the agents who represent qualified buyers. The first two weeks on the open market generate the most qualified attention a listing will ever receive, so entering with correct pricing and a finished marketing package matters more than a long, slow campaign. In fast moving pockets like Washington Park, where homes sold in roughly 15 days in spring 2026, preparation before launch is decisive.[6] Pricing should be built from active and pending sales within the same neighborhood and price band, because sophisticated buyers read an overpriced listing as weakness and respond with low offers.

Preparing a luxury home to sell

Preparation is where luxury sellers protect both price and timeline. A pre listing inspection surfaces issues before a buyer’s inspector does, which removes a common renegotiation lever. Staging and professional media, including photography, video, drone, and a 3D walkthrough, are baseline expectations at this price point, not extras. Finishing repairs and assembling disclosures, surveys, and HOA or covenant documents before launch keeps the transaction moving once an offer arrives. The objective is a listing that looks complete and credible on day one, so the first two weeks of attention convert into strong offers rather than questions.

The closing process for a Denver luxury purchase

A financed Denver luxury purchase typically runs 30 to 45 days from accepted offer to closing, with several milestones along the way. Earnest money is deposited, the title commitment and HOA or covenant documents are reviewed, and the inspection objection and resolution period sets the tone for the deal. On a jumbo loan the appraisal carries extra weight, since a value below the contract price can require renegotiation or additional cash to close. Loan conditions are cleared, a final walkthrough confirms condition, and funds and title transfer at closing. Building realistic time into each step, and lining up financing early, keeps a high stakes purchase on schedule.

How long do luxury homes take to sell in Denver?

Time on market depends heavily on neighborhood and price band. Walkable, turnkey homes in Cherry Creek often sell in under a month[3], and Washington Park has run near 15 days in competitive periods.[6] Larger estates in Cherry Hills Village are lower in volume and more sensitive to precise pricing, with listing medians around 40 days.[4] Inventory also varies by tier, which shifts leverage: in early 2026 detached homes between $1 million and $1.49 million carried under four months of supply, while higher bands carried far more.[1] For a current read on your block, a local agent can pull active and pending comparables and translate them into a timing plan.

Schools and amenities, and how to research them

Buyers frequently ask about schools when comparing Denver neighborhoods. School assignments, attendance boundaries, ratings, and enrollment policies change over time, so confirm them directly with the relevant school district and objective third party sources such as GreatSchools[13] for any specific address before relying on them. On amenities, Denver’s luxury neighborhoods offer features that are easy to verify in person: the Cherry Creek shopping and dining district, the trail along Cherry Creek, Washington Park and the High Line Canal path, and quick access to downtown and the Denver Tech Center. Touring at different times of day is the most reliable way to judge noise, traffic, and the feel of a specific block.

Rising inventory, up about 9 percent year over year in February 2026[2], gives buyers more selection and negotiating room than in tighter years, especially in price bands carrying more months of supply. Sellers of well prepared, correctly priced homes in liquid neighborhoods still transact quickly.
